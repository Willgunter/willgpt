## 1. Explicit assumptions in the text

### Electrical environment and voltage bounds

* **Motors are assumed to be safe for continuous operation within ±10% of rating**: ESF motors designed to “operate continuously, without harm” at **±10%** of rated voltage.

* **Maximum and minimum voltages are treated as “design requirements”**, but **sustained minimum voltage is explicitly declared not credible** at DCPP due to grid controls and fast transfers. 

### Protective device behavior and setpoint methodology

* **Thermal overload relay (49) trip setpoint is assumed nominally 125% of the minimum heater element value** from manufacturer tables. 
* **Thermal overload relay manufacturing tolerance is +0 / −15%** (trip can be 15% lower than nominal). 
* **Example numeric assumption**:

  * Motor FLA = **14.8 A**
  * Heater range = **13.6–14.9 A**
  * Nominal trip = **1.25 × 13.6 = 17 A**
  * Minimum trip with −15% tolerance = **0.85 × 17 = 14.45 A**, which is below 14.8 A. 

### Motor current under undervoltage

* **At 90% voltage, motor requires ~10% more current than rated full-load current** (explicit approximation). 

### Time characteristics and credibility of undervoltage

* **Thermal overload relays require about 15–20 minutes to operate** in the relevant ampere range, so undervoltage must persist that long to cause a trip. 
* **Transmission system control practices** (minimum 500 kV and 230 kV maintained, load shedding, excitation, separation logic) are assumed to prevent sustained undervoltage; worst case is “over in seconds.” 

### Ambient temperature basis

* Overload relay curves are based on **40°C ambient**, with a stated correction: **1% additional margin per °C below 40°C**; DCPP MCC temperature **35–37°C**, giving **3–5% extra margin**. 

### Circuit breaker instantaneous trip behavior

* Breakers (52) must be set to avoid nuisance trip on **motor inrush** at **maximum voltage**. 
* During starting, they assume **motor current is proportional to bus voltage** and use a **constant impedance** model during starting. 
* Starting inrush is stated as **more than 6× normal current**, producing **6–12% voltage drop** at start. 
* High bus voltage accident scenarios are explicitly ruled out because accident buses transfer to startup bus and load flow studies show bus voltages “a few volts less than 460 V.” 

### Scope and outcomes

* **65 thermal overload relays** (including non-Class 1E) did not meet worst-case requirements; **77 Unit 1** and **69 Unit 2** safety-related breakers needed new setpoints. 
* Report asserts **no safety function was inadvertently tripped** and safe operation was assured due to non-credible sustained extremes.

## 3. First-principles laws governing the described system

### 3.1 Ohm’s law and impedance-based current relations

* **Law**: ( I = \frac{V}{Z} ) (AC steady-state magnitude form).
* **Application here**:

  * For **motor starting**, the report uses a **constant impedance** assumption: higher bus voltage gives proportionally higher inrush current. 
* **Validity regime**:

  * Approximate for induction motor locked-rotor behavior over small ranges, assuming impedance magnitude roughly constant during the start transient.
* **Boundary/violation risks**:

  * Induction motor impedance is not strictly constant because rotor frequency, saturation, and slip evolve during acceleration. At high voltages, magnetic saturation reduces effective inductive reactance, making current rise faster than linear.

### 3.2 Electromechanical power balance for induction motors

* **Principle**: Electrical input power (P_{in} \approx 3 V I \cos\phi); mechanical output (P_{mech} = T \omega). Losses convert to heat.
* **Application**:

  * Under undervoltage, to deliver required torque/power, current tends to increase, raising heating in windings and also in protective devices. The report approximates **~10% more current at 90% voltage**. 
* **Validity**:

  * Near rated operating region; depends on load torque curve and motor slip.
* **Boundary effects**:

  * For loads with torque that rises strongly with speed (fans), undervoltage can prevent reaching operating point, increasing slip and heating dramatically.

### 3.3 Joule heating and thermal integration in heater elements

* **Law**: (P_{heat} = I^2 R). Thermal energy accumulation ( \Delta Q = \int P_{heat},dt ).
* **Application**:

  * Heater elements convert current to heat; relay actuates when accumulated thermal condition reaches threshold. 
* **Validity**:

  * Dominant mechanism in metallic heaters and bimetal systems.
* **Boundary effects**:

  * Resistance (R) varies with temperature (R(T)), altering heating rate.
  * Heat dissipation depends on ambient and airflow; the report explicitly references ambient temperature basis and correction. 

### 3.4 Heat transfer and thermal time constants

* **Principle**: Lumped thermal model ( C_{th}\frac{dT}{dt} = I^2R - hA(T-T_{amb}) ) (simplified).
* **Application**:

  * Explains why overload trips require **15–20 minutes** in the relevant overcurrent band. 
* **Validity**:

  * Lumped capacitance approximation if temperature gradients inside the heater are not dominant.
* **Boundary effects**:

  * If the heater is not well-lumped, local hot spots can trigger earlier than predicted.

### 3.5 Protection coordination and time-current curves

* **Principle**: Protective devices must coordinate so that downstream devices clear faults before upstream devices, and so that start transients do not cause nuisance trips.
* **Application**:

  * Breakers must be set high enough to tolerate starting inrush at max voltage, but low enough to isolate faults. The report frames the setpoint errors as failing to envelope voltage extremes plus tolerances. 
* **Boundary effects**:

  * If setpoints are raised to avoid nuisance trips, short-circuit clearing times increase, raising arc-flash energy and equipment thermal stress.

## 3. First principles governing the system

Even though the text is short, the physical regime implied is rich. These are the governing principles for foam bonded to a substrate in cryogenic and ascent environments.

### 3.1 Heat transfer (conduction, convection, radiation)

* **Law**: Fourier’s law for conduction
  [
  \mathbf{q} = -k \nabla T
  ]
* **Application**: foam is used as **cryogenic insulation**, so thermal gradients across foam thickness drive heat leak and create internal thermal strains.
* **Validity regime**: steady or transient conduction in porous media; effective conductivity (k_\text{eff}) depends on cell gas, radiation within pores, and solid fraction.
* **Boundary/violations**:

  * At very low temperatures, gas conductivity drops, radiative contribution changes; (k_\text{eff}(T)) can be strongly temperature dependent.
  * If foam cracks or de-bonds create air gaps, heat transfer shifts (contact resistance, convection in voids).

### 3.2 Thermoelasticity and differential thermal contraction

* **Law**: thermal strain
  [
  \varepsilon_\text{th} = \alpha \Delta T
  ]
  and stress in constrained systems (linear elastic approximation)
  [
  \sigma \approx E(\varepsilon - \alpha\Delta T)
  ]
* **Application**: foam bonded to a much stiffer substrate experiences **constraint**. During cryogenic chill-down, foam shrinks; if constrained, it develops tensile/compressive stresses and interfacial shear/peel stresses that promote de-bonding.
* **Validity regime**: small strains, linear elastic or viscoelastic depending on temperature.
* **Boundary/violations**:

  * Polyurethane is **viscoelastic** and temperature dependent; at cryogenic temperatures it can behave glassy and brittle, making linear elastic fracture more relevant.
  * Large (\Delta T) can cause non-linear behavior and microcracking.

### 3.3 Cellular solids mechanics

* **Law**: foam modulus and strength scale with relative density (Gibson–Ashby type scaling)
  [
  E \propto E_s \left(\frac{\rho}{\rho_s}\right)^n
  ]
* **Application**: “cell morphology” directly influences stiffness, crack paths, and failure modes. Cell wall thickness, anisotropy, and defects alter local stress concentrations.
* **Validity regime**: quasi-static loading; depends on open/closed cell structure and deformation mechanism (bending vs stretching).
* **Boundary/violations**:

  * Under dynamic loads (acoustics, vibration), rate effects matter.
  * Cryogenic temperatures may shift deformation mode to brittle cell wall fracture.

### 3.4 Fracture mechanics and interfacial delamination

* **Law**: energy release rate criterion for crack growth
  [
  G \ge G_c
  ]
  with mixed-mode interfacial fracture (Mode I opening + Mode II shear).
* **Application**: de-bonding is interfacial crack growth driven by thermal mismatch stresses, mechanical loads, and defects.
* **Validity regime**: LEFM when material is brittle and process zone small; cohesive zone models when large-scale yielding or damage occurs.
* **Boundary/violations**:

  * Foam has distributed damage; crack tip may be diffuse.
  * Mixed-mode fracture toughness depends on temperature, strain rate, moisture content.

### 3.5 Moisture transport and freeze-thaw (if applicable)

* **Law**: diffusion (Fick’s law)
  [
  \mathbf{J} = -D \nabla c
  ]
* **Application**: moisture absorption can alter polyurethane properties and create internal pressures on freezing.
* **Validity regime**: porous diffusion; depends on cell connectivity.
* **Boundary/violations**:

  * Microcracks create fast pathways.
  * Cyclic humidity and temperature can accelerate degradation.

## 4. Interdependencies of first principles (how they interact)

### Thermal gradients ↔ mechanical stress ↔ fracture

* **Fourier conduction** establishes (\nabla T).
* (\nabla T) drives **thermal strain** (\alpha\Delta T).
* Bond constraint converts strain into **interfacial stresses** (peel/shear).
* Those stresses drive **delamination** via (G).

This is the central coupled mechanism: heat transfer sets the thermal state, thermoelasticity sets stresses, fracture mechanics determines de-bond progression.

### Cellular morphology ↔ stress concentrations ↔ crack path

* Cell size gradients, anisotropy, and voids create local stiffness variations.
* Those variations concentrate stress at the interface and within the foam, changing whether failure is:

  * Cohesive in foam
  * Adhesive at interface
  * Mixed

### Time dependence: viscoelastic relaxation ↔ cyclic loading ↔ fatigue-like damage

* Polyurethane viscoelasticity can relax stresses during dwell.
* But repeated cycles can create microdamage accumulation (foam “fatigue”).
* Relaxation can be protective in one regime and harmful in another (creep opening interfacial flaws).
