1. Introduction
Pulsed xenon lamps, with the advantages of short-duration highenergy output and broad-spectrum characteristics, have been widely
applied in the medical [1,2] and energy fields [3,4]. In the medical
field, pulsed xenon lamps have been widely applied in photothermal skin therapies and aesthetic treatments [1,5,6]. Clinically, pulsed
xenon lamp is mainly used for superficial skin disorders and skin
rejuvenation [7â€“14]. More recently, their utilization has expanded to
a broader range of energy-related applications, such as energy storage
and beyond [15â€“19]. Within the energy sector, their most prominent
application lies in solar cell material processing, where pulsed xenon
lamps play a key role in advancing solar energy conversion technologies [20â€“22]. Specifically, pulsed xenon irradiation enables effective
annealing of thin-film absorber layers and the sintering of printed metal
contacts, while minimizing thermal damage to fragile thin substrates.
With its efficient materials processing capability, pulsed xenon technology is expected to accelerate the large-scale manufacturing of solar
cells [3,4,20,21]. These capabilities provide significant advantages over

systematic studies on the optical behavior, structural design methods, and engineering application potential of multi-light-source pulsed

configurations, the effects of radiation superposition and structural
parameters such as the number, geometric dimensions, and spatial
arrangement of the lamps on the resulting energy field distribution
remain insufficiently understood in the existing literature. In summary,
the existing pulsed xenon lamp system designs have two main shortcomings: the first is a lack of effective guidance for the divergent light
from the sources, and the second is the absence of systematic studies
on multi-light-source integration.
Naturally, whether to optimize the structure of pulsed xenon lamps
to reduce inherent drawbacks, or to accurately predict their radiative
energy distribution for precise energy control [24,25] or avoiding side
effects in clinical treatments [26â€“28], there is an urgent need for precise
models to predict the radiation energy output in pulsed xenon lamp
systems.
Typically, there are two approaches for predicting the radiation energy of divergent light sources: one involves using commercial optical
software based on ray-tracing principles, while the other computes the
radiation energy distribution by solving the Radiative Transfer Equation (RTE) [29â€“34]. However, commercial optical software often relies
on external CAD tools to model practical application scenarios. This
dependence not only complicates the workflow and limits flexibility in
making modifications but also reduces the systemâ€™s ability to respond
efficiently to rapidly changing or complex operating conditions [35].
Moreover, the high cost of such software presents a significant barrier for researchers who are not specialists in optics. Coupled with
steep learning requirements and substantial computational demands,
these factors further discourage use by those outside the optics field.
Nevertheless, obtaining accurate solutions to the RTE is intrinsically
difficult because it represents a high-dimensional integro-differential
equation, necessitating the use of approximation techniques for numerical evaluation [36,37]. Consequently, determining the radiative energy
distribution from the RTE remains a complex task for researchers who
are not experts in the field, with beginners frequently encountering
mistakes or misapplying methods during the modeling process. The
Monte Carlo ray-tracing technique, as a well-established approach,
has been widely employed for predicting the energy distribution of
divergent light sources [38â€“40]. Moreover, predictive models based
on Monte Carlo ray-tracing typically offer high-throughput data capabilities, enabling rapid database construction and facilitating the
integration of advanced analytical approaches such as deep learning
in subsequent studies, thereby further optimizing system design and
enhancing performance. However, in previous studies, the accuracy of
Monte Carlo ray-tracing predictions of radiative energy distribution was
typically validated only at selected key points on the receiving surface,
with few investigations assessing the accuracy of the predicted energy
distribution across the entire spatial domain [41,42].
In this study, to overcome the structural limitations of conventional pulsed xenon lamp systems and meet practical application requirements, our research team has proposed a novel pulsed xenon
lamp system, comprising a closed reflective cavity, pulsed xenon lamp
sources, and a light-guiding structure. Meanwhile, based on the Monte
Carlo ray-tracing method, we developed a pulsed xenon lamp radiation
energy prediction model suitable for multi-lamp systems. Subsequently,
the accuracy of global radiation energy predictions was validated on
receiving surfaces at various distances. The modelâ€™s accuracy was rigorously validated through comparisons with commercial optical software,
demonstrating its reliability in predicting complex radiative patterns.
By exploiting the data generation capability of the model, we further
analyzed how source positions and structural parameters influence the
overall performance of pulsed xenon lamp systems, providing valuable insights into system optimization. This work provides a simple,
efficient, and practical approach to addressing the shortcomings of
conventional pulsed xenon lamp structures. In addition, a flexible,
user-friendly, highly scalable, and cost-effective predictive model was
developed to precisely control the radiative energy distribution within
pulsed xenon lamp systems, providing guidance for practical applications, enhancing production efficiency, and reducing the occurrence of
adverse events.

In this work, at the initial step, photons are evenly emitted from
each light source. Then, the propagation of photons within the pulsed
xenon lamp system after emission is categorized into three sequential
stages: interaction with the cavity, interaction with the lens, and absorption on the receiving surface, as shown in Fig. 2. The first stage
corresponds to cavity interaction. Once a photon is randomly emitted
from the light source, it is first checked whether its initial trajectory
allows it to directly escape the reflective chamber and strike the top
surface of the lens. If instead the photon collides with the cavity wall,
the intersection point is logged, and its new direction is obtained using
the reflection vector. The updated path is then re-examined to see
whether the photon can leave the cavity. This loop continues until the
photon is either absorbed or successfully exits the chamber. The second
stage involves lens interaction. Here, the photonâ€™s ability to either pass
straight through the lens to the receiving surface or undergo an indirect
transmission is assessed. The point at which the photon meets the upper
surface of the lens is recorded, followed by calculation of the refraction
vector to determine whether it can arrive at the bottom surface. If the
photon fails to reach the lower surface directly, it may strike the lateral
surfaces of the lens and experience total internal reflection. In such
cases, the reflected trajectory is traced iteratively until the photon either escapes or is terminated. The third stage corresponds to interaction
with the receiving surface. Once the photon exits the lens from the
lower surface, a new refraction vector is computed to check whether
it can strike the receiving plane. If successful, the photonâ€™s impact
location is stored, and its energy contribution at that point on the
receiving surface is calculated. In this model, the geometric structure is
generated synchronously with the light-source model, with geometric
boundaries created based on predefined geometric parameters. The
specific details are illustrated in the flowchart.

2.3. Experimental scheme
In this section, an experimental validation of the predicted radiative
energy distribution in both space and time is performed. The newly developed pulsed xenon lamp system was constructed with an aluminum
alloy circular cavity and a K9 glass cuboid lens. The xenon lamp,
supplied by Excelitas, was positioned such that its center coincided with
the origin of the coordinate system. The structural dimensions of the
experimental setup were kept identical to those used in the numerical
model. Because of the limited receiving area and the rapid variation
of radiative energy within a few milliseconds, direct measurement of
the energy evolution is challenging. Hence, the model validation was
carried out through a comparison of the corresponding temperature
fields. A high-speed infrared camera (TELOPS SPARK IR), operating
at a frame interval of 2 ms, was employed to capture the transient
temperature variations on the rear surface of an aluminum plate coated
with a black alumina layer. The emissivity of the aluminum plate is
set to 0.91. The aluminum plate measures 100 Ã— 100 mm in size,
with a thickness of 0.5 mm, and is coated with a black alumina layer
approximately 8 Î¼m thick. In the experiment, the pulsed xenon lamp
was operated at 170 kW with an 8 ms pulse duration. This power value
was calculated by dividing the energy emitted in a single pulse by the
pulse width. At the same time, a photodetector (PDA36a2, Thorlabs)

4. Conclusion
At present, Pulsed xenon lamps have been widely applied in fields
such as energy and medicine. However, existing pulsed xenon lamp
system designs suffer from multiple structural shortcomings, which hinder the effective guidance of the divergent light emitted by the lamps,
resulting in low energy utilization efficiency. In addition, current systems typically employ a single light source, with little exploration of
the performance of multi-light-source configurations. And in multilight-source systems, a comprehensive understanding of how lamp
geometry, spatial arrangement, and overall structural parameters interact to influence system performance is still lacking, and systematic
investigations on these coupled effects remain scarce. Moreover, there
is a lack of simple and efficient predictive models for radiative energy,
which are needed to guide the practical use of pulsed xenon lamps
in production and applications. To overcome these limitations, this
study first developed a novel multi-source pulsed xenon lamp system,
designed to achieve effective guidance of the divergent light. The
system consists of a closed reflective cavity, pulsed xenon lamp sources,
and a light-guiding structure. In addition, a radiative energy prediction
model for multi-lamp systems was constructed based on ray-tracing
Monte Carlo simulations. The model was employed to predict the
spatial distribution of radiative energy in both the two-light-source and
three-light-source systems. Based on this model, the effects of multisource geometric parameters, spatial arrangement, and overall system
structure on system performance were also investigated. Comparison
with commercial optical software showed that the prediction error of
radiative energy distribution was within 4%. Additional experimental
results further supported and confirmed the accuracy and reliability
of the proposed model. Based on data generated by this model, the
effects of lamp radius and length on the performance of the two-lightsource system were investigated. Furthermore, the influence of lamp
